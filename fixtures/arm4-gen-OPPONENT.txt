ID: OPPONENT-1
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Coordination demand compresses into the remaining four days.
REASONING: Total demand is unchanged; the same hours squeezed into busier days deepen fragmentation where the week is worst.
DEFEATER: Calendar data from firms with the policy showing total meeting hours fell.

ID: OPPONENT-2
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Incidents and urgent decisions do not respect a calendar ban.
REASONING: Escalations need same-day synchronous coordination; the ban is violated immediately or delays response.
DEFEATER: An explicit incident-response exemption collapses the objection.

ID: OPPONENT-3
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: External counterparties lose access one day in five.
REASONING: Customers, partners and candidates schedule on their own calendars; a hard internal ban forces staff to break policy or lose business.
DEFEATER: An exemption for external-facing meetings removes the harm.

ID: OPPONENT-4
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: A blanket ban trades coordination-heavy functions' output for focus-heavy comfort.
REASONING: Sales, support and program management run on synchronous contact and pay the price of a uniform rule.
DEFEATER: If per-function exemptions are allowed, the policy is no longer a ban.

ID: OPPONENT-5
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Coordination shifts into invisible channels that no calendar metric sees.
REASONING: Chat threads and ad-hoc calls replace scheduled meetings; reported hours fall while interruption load rises.
DEFEATER: Interruption telemetry showing no rise in unscheduled contact on banned days.

ID: OPPONENT-6
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Junior staff lose scheduled access to seniors first.
REASONING: Mentoring and unblocking happen in booked slots; cutting a day of slots cuts the least powerful people's access.
DEFEATER: Evidence that mentoring reliably reschedules rather than disappears.

ID: OPPONENT-7
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: The ban treats a symptom of bad meeting culture that will resurface on four days.
REASONING: Low-value meetings come from unclear owners and agendas, which prohibition of a day does not touch.
DEFEATER: If the ban measurably reduces total low-value hours, symptom relief is still relief.

ID: OPPONENT-8
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: A specialist in HR would add: enforcement creates recurring policy-conflict overhead.
REASONING: Exception requests and disputes are ongoing labour that projections routinely understate.
DEFEATER: Fully automated calendar enforcement drives the recurring cost toward zero.
