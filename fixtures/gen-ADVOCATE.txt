ID: ADVOCATE-1
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: Banning Friday meetings protects a weekly block of deep work.
REASONING: Knowledge work needs long uninterrupted stretches. A guaranteed meeting-free day removes the scheduling fragmentation that destroys them.
DEFEATER: Evidence that fragmented calendars do not reduce measured output for most roles.

ID: ADVOCATE-2
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: Context switching around meetings costs more than the meetings themselves.
REASONING: Each meeting imposes preparation and recovery overhead on every attendee. Removing one day of meetings removes a full day of that overhead.
DEFEATER: A time-use study showing recovery overhead is trivially small in practice.

ID: ADVOCATE-3
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: A ban forces meeting hygiene on the other four days.
REASONING: Scarcity makes organisers justify each slot. Recurring low-value meetings are the first to be cut.
DEFEATER: If meetings simply migrate to Monday-Thursday with no net reduction, the hygiene effect is zero.

ID: ADVOCATE-4
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: Meeting-free Fridays are a cheap, visible recruiting and retention perk.
REASONING: Candidates weigh predictable focus time. The policy costs nothing to announce and signals respect for makers' time.
DEFEATER: Surveys showing candidates rank such perks far below pay and remote flexibility.

ID: ADVOCATE-5
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: The ban pushes teams toward written, asynchronous decision records.
REASONING: When you cannot meet, you must write. Written decisions are searchable and auditable later.
DEFEATER: If decision quality measurably drops without live discussion, async records are a poor substitute.

ID: ADVOCATE-6
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: A fixed no-meeting day is fairer across time zones.
REASONING: Global teams burden someone's evening with every synchronous slot. One protected day spares everyone equally.
DEFEATER: Timezone pain is concentrated on a few staff who might prefer a different remedy entirely.

ID: ADVOCATE-7
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: Fridays are the lowest-energy day, so the ban wastes the least collaboration value.
REASONING: Attention and decision quality decline late in the week. Scheduling collaboration earlier aligns effort with energy.
DEFEATER: Chronotype data showing no systematic Friday dip in this workforce.

ID: ADVOCATE-8
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: A specialist in operations would add: the ban simplifies room and calendar capacity planning.
REASONING: Peak demand for rooms and video bridges flattens when one day is removed from contention.
DEFEATER: If Friday demand simply shifts and raises Monday-Thursday peak load, capacity planning gets harder, not easier.
