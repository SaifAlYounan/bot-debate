ID: SKEPTIC-1
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: The question is undefined until 'meeting' is defined.
REASONING: A two-person pairing session, a client call and a 40-person status readout are different objects. Any ban conflates them and produces incoherent enforcement.
DEFEATER: A policy text with a crisp operational definition of the banned object.

ID: SKEPTIC-2
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: Ban-versus-no-ban is a false binary.
REASONING: Meeting budgets, default-25-minute slots, no-meeting mornings and approval thresholds all exist. Framing it as Friday prohibition hides the real design space.
DEFEATER: Evidence the binary policy outperforms every graded alternative on the same metric.

ID: SKEPTIC-3
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: The proposal optimises an input metric, inviting Goodhart effects.
REASONING: Meeting hours are an input, not an outcome. Banning an input predictably spawns substitutes: 'working sessions', huddles, calls that are meetings in all but name.
DEFEATER: If outcome metrics improve alongside the input metric, the Goodhart worry is empirically idle.

ID: SKEPTIC-4
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: Cited focus-time studies rarely survive selection-effect scrutiny.
REASONING: Firms that adopt meeting-free days differ systematically from firms that do not. Their before-after gains cannot be read as the policy's causal effect.
DEFEATER: A randomised or quasi-experimental study isolating the policy effect.

ID: SKEPTIC-5
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: Nobody has said who enforces the ban or what a violation costs.
REASONING: A rule without an enforcement mechanism is a slogan. A rule with one has costs nobody has priced.
DEFEATER: A published enforcement design whose costs are visibly below the claimed benefits.

ID: SKEPTIC-6
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: 'Should offices ban' presumes the office is the right decision level.
REASONING: Team-level, department-level and individual-level versions of the policy exist. The question smuggles in centralisation before argument starts.
DEFEATER: A governance analysis showing office-level uniformity beats local choice here.

ID: SKEPTIC-7
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: A pilot is being debated as if it were a permanent policy.
REASONING: The rational move under uncertainty is a time-boxed reversible trial with pre-registered metrics. Arguing the permanent question first is premature.
DEFEATER: If switching costs make even a pilot effectively irreversible, the distinction dissolves.

ID: SKEPTIC-8
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: A specialist in survey methodology would add: employee-preference data on meetings is systematically unreliable.
REASONING: Stated preferences about meetings diverge from revealed scheduling behaviour. Both sides quote surveys that cannot bear the weight.
DEFEATER: A preference elicitation with real stakes that replicates the survey result.
