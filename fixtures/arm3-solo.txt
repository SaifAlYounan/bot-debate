ID: SOLO-1
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: A meeting-free Friday protects deep work by removing calendar fragmentation.
REASONING: Long uninterrupted stretches are the binding input for knowledge work. One guaranteed day changes the weekly equilibrium.
DEFEATER: Evidence that fragmented calendars do not reduce measured output for most roles.

ID: SOLO-2
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: Constraining meeting supply forces justification of every remaining slot.
REASONING: Scarcity pushes organisers to cut recurring low-value meetings first.
DEFEATER: Calendar data showing meetings migrate wholesale to other days instead.

ID: SOLO-3
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: The ban accelerates a written, asynchronous decision culture.
REASONING: When you cannot meet you must write, and written decisions are searchable later.
DEFEATER: If decision quality measurably drops without live discussion, async is a poor substitute.

ID: SOLO-4
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: A fixed quiet day is fairer across time zones than rolling synchronous load.
REASONING: Global teams burden someone's evening with every sync; one protected day spares all equally.
DEFEATER: Timezone pain concentrates on few staff who might prefer a different remedy.

ID: SOLO-5
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Coordination demand compresses into Monday-Thursday.
REASONING: Total demand is unchanged; squeezing it into four days worsens the busiest days.
DEFEATER: Calendar studies showing total meeting hours fall under day bans.

ID: SOLO-6
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Customers and partners lose access one day in five.
REASONING: External parties schedule on their own calendars; a hard ban forces staff to break policy or lose business.
DEFEATER: An explicit exemption for external-facing meetings.

ID: SOLO-7
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Incident response requires same-day synchronous coordination.
REASONING: Escalations do not respect calendars; the ban is violated immediately or delays response.
DEFEATER: An explicit incident-response exemption collapses the objection.

ID: SOLO-8
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Coordination-heavy functions pay for focus-heavy functions' benefit.
REASONING: Sales, support and program management run on synchronous contact; a uniform rule trades their output away.
DEFEATER: Function-level data showing all teams gain net output under the ban.

ID: SOLO-9
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: An economist would add: a price mechanism dominates a quantity ban.
REASONING: Meeting budgets let high-value meetings survive and kill low-value ones; bans cannot discriminate on value.
DEFEATER: Transaction costs of a budget scheme exceeding its allocative gains.

ID: SOLO-10
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: 'Meeting' is undefined, so the proposition is not yet decidable.
REASONING: A pairing session, a client call and a 40-person readout are different objects; a ban conflates them.
DEFEATER: A policy text with a crisp operational definition.

ID: SOLO-11
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: The right decision procedure is a time-boxed pilot with pre-registered metrics.
REASONING: The empirical disputes — compression, substitution, external harm — are all answerable by a cheap trial.
DEFEATER: If pilot effects are contaminated or irreversible, the trial answers nothing.

ID: SOLO-12
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: A missing stakeholder — the customer — should have representation in the decision.
REASONING: Outsiders bear real costs but have no seat; their response determines much of the policy's actual cost.
DEFEATER: If external-facing meetings are exempt, outsiders are barely affected.
