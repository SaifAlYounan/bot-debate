ID: LENS_A-1
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: The marginal meeting has negative expected value once attendee time is priced.
REASONING: Multiply attendance by loaded hourly cost and many recurring meetings visibly destroy value. A ban prunes the negative tail cheaply.
DEFEATER: Portfolio data showing the Friday meeting mix is not disproportionately low-value.

ID: LENS_A-2
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: A price mechanism beats a quantity ban.
REASONING: Charging organisers an internal meeting budget lets high-value meetings continue and kills low-value ones. Quantity bans cannot discriminate on value.
DEFEATER: If transaction costs of running a meeting-budget scheme exceed its allocative gains, the blunt ban wins.

ID: LENS_A-3
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Coordination has externalities a uniform ban ignores.
REASONING: One team's cancelled sync delays three dependent teams. The ban prices none of these cross-team effects.
DEFEATER: Dependency-mapping data showing Friday syncs sit on few critical paths.

ID: LENS_A-4
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: Focus time is a public good that individual scheduling under-provides.
REASONING: Any one person accepting a meeting imposes fragmentation on shared work. A collective rule solves the assurance problem individuals cannot.
DEFEATER: Evidence that informal norms already protect focus blocks without a rule.

ID: LENS_A-5
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: The relevant quantity is elasticity: how much meeting demand shifts versus disappears.
REASONING: If demand mostly shifts to Thursday, welfare gain is near zero. The debate should centre on measured substitution rates.
DEFEATER: A natural experiment measuring substitution close to zero would settle this against the reframe.

ID: LENS_A-6
SIDE: AGAINST
CLAIM: Compliance and monitoring costs recur forever and compound.
REASONING: Every exception request, audit and dispute is labour. Steady-state administrative cost routinely exceeds first-year projections for blanket rules.
DEFEATER: If enforcement is fully automated in calendar tooling, recurring cost approaches zero.

ID: LENS_A-7
SIDE: FOR
CLAIM: The policy is a cheap commitment device against time-inconsistent scheduling.
REASONING: Organisations systematically over-book future weeks. A standing rule pre-commits against a bias everyone acknowledges.
DEFEATER: Evidence that scheduling discipline can be achieved by defaults without prohibition.

ID: LENS_A-8
SIDE: REFRAME
CLAIM: A specialist in labour economics would add: measure output, not meetings.
REASONING: Hours in meetings is an input proxy with weak correlation to productivity. Policy should target measured output per team.
DEFEATER: If output is unmeasurable at the team level in this firm, input proxies are all that is actionable.
